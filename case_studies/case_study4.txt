FINDING 1 — COMPOSITE SLAB WITH UNSEPARATED MATERIALS

One floor slab carried the name:

Floor — Concrete Deck — Tapered Insulation

The element contains an IfcMaterialConstituentSet with:

rigid insulation
cast-in-place concrete (28 MPa)

No material fractions are defined for either constituent. The IFC therefore provides no deterministic split between materials.

Quantity evaluation:

NetVolume: 828.272 m³
NetArea: 1622.195 m²
Implied average thickness: 0.511 m

This thickness is not consistent with standard structural concrete deck behavior and indicates insulation-dominated geometry within a composite system.

The element cannot be reliably decomposed into concrete vs insulation portions from IFC data alone. It is therefore excluded from the concrete BOQ and flagged for QS review.

The IFC does not contain incorrect data — it contains inseparable combined material representation.

FINDING 2 — STRUCTURAL STEEL COLUMNS WITHOUT MATERIAL ASSIGNMENT

Seven UC356x368x129 columns contain no explicit material definition:

no material name
no material assignment
no reference Pset classification

Classification was derived from geometric signal:

UC356x368x129 CrossSectionArea: 0.016235 m²
450mm concrete column CrossSectionArea: 0.159020 m²

The order-of-magnitude difference provides a reliable structural classification signal even in the absence of material metadata.

All seven elements were correctly excluded from the concrete BOQ.

Without this signal-based filtering, approximately 0.414 m³ of steel geometry would have been incorrectly included in concrete quantities.

FINDING 3 — BEAM VOLUME MODIFIED BY EXPORT BEHAVIOR

One 300 × 600 mm concrete beam spanning 7.3 m:

Theoretical solid volume: 1.317 m³
IFC NetVolume: 0.621 m³
Difference: 0.696 m³ (53%)

This reduction is caused by slab-beam intersection handling during export. Revit removes intersecting volumes from beam elements and retains them in slab elements to prevent double counting.

The IFC therefore represents net constructible volume rather than theoretical solid extrusion.

The pipeline uses NetVolume directly for all beam calculations without additional correction.

FINDING 4 — NAMING VS GEOMETRY DISCREPANCY IN PILE CAPS

One pile cap labeled:

800 × 1800 × 900 mm

Observed data:

Name: 900 mm depth
BaseQuantities Depth: 1200 mm
IFC NetVolume confirms 1200 mm geometry

Additionally, four four-pile caps at perimeter locations are trimmed by site boundary conditions. Their actual modeled volumes differ from nominal naming dimensions.

A name-based takeoff would overestimate pile cap concrete by 1.137 m³ across the set.

The pipeline relies exclusively on BaseQuantities-derived geometry. Naming conventions are not used for quantity derivation.

WHY THIS MATTERS

At production scale, IFC errors do not appear as failures. They appear as plausible outputs.

insulation behaves like concrete in composite slabs
steel elements appear structurally identical without material tags
beam volumes reflect export logic rather than geometry intent
pile cap names diverge from modeled geometry
reinforcement exists only partially in structured attributes

None of these trigger extraction errors.

They propagate silently into BOQs, cost plans, and reinforcement schedules.

IFC quantity extraction at scale is not a calculation problem. It is a validation problem across inconsistent and partially defined semantic layers.

The critical skill is not reading IFC data — it is distinguishing between what is explicitly defined, what is inferred, and what is structurally absent.
