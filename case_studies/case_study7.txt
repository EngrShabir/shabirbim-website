CASE STUDY 07
CPS-026/270 — Residential RC Frame
Revit 2021 · IFC2X3 · Real project file · Kenya

THE PROBLEM
This is not a benchmark model. Not a test file. A real residential reinforced concrete frame in Kenya.
The first delivery failed.
StructBOQ steel — 20,906 kg
Correct steel — 2,409 kg
Error — 767%
The issue was not missing data.
The data was fully present in the IFC.
The system simply did not read it.
That failure triggered a full re-analysis of every element, assumption, and extraction path.

WHAT THE FILE CONTAINED
1,484 total IFC elements
209 concrete structural elements
122.801 m³ validated concrete volume
91 columns — 2 section types, 2 height variants
52 beams — 3 types
41 pad footings — IfcSlab exported as BASESLAB
18 strip footings — no BaseQuantities
4 concrete slabs — including one 3-layer ground floor construction
2 stair flights with landings
18 masonry walls — excluded
1,252 IfcReinforcingBar elements — fully modeled rebar system

FINDING 1 — COMPLETE REBAR MODEL NOT EXTRACTED IN INITIAL RUN
Initial result — 20,906 kg — ratio-based estimate.
Correct result — 2,409.84 kg — geometry-based.
The rebar model was complete. Every bar individually modeled. Not one diameter missing. Not one length absent.
1,252 IfcReinforcingBar elements. NominalDiameter present. BarLength present. CrossSectionArea present on all 1,252.
Correct computation — CrossSectionArea × BarLength × 7850 kg/m³ per bar.
T8 — 457 bars — 348.47 kg
T10 — 657 bars — 1,572.51 kg
T12 — 114 bars — 242.33 kg
T16 — 24 bars — 246.54 kg
Total — 2,409.84 kg
Failure cause — rebar extraction feature not implemented in first engine. System defaulted to concrete volume ratio method. The data was present. The extraction path was not.

FINDING 2 — MULTI-LAYER SLAB MISINTERPRETED AS FULL CONCRETE VOLUME
Ground floor slab — Floor:150mm Solid Slab With A142 Mesh.
IFC NetVolume — 60.2249 m³.
Actual build-up — 100mm concrete structural, 50mm sand blinding, 300mm earth subgrade. Total modeled thickness — 450mm.
The entire 450mm was interpreted as concrete.
Correct concrete volume — 13.383 m³.
Overstatement — 46.842 m³ of sand and earth incorrectly priced as concrete.
Cost impact — €5,621 at €120 per m³. From one slab. One family. One unchecked assumption.
This pattern appears in residential construction worldwide. Any ground floor family modeled with blinding, sand, or subgrade layers carries this risk. The engine now detects IfcMaterialLayerSetUsage, identifies the structural concrete layer, and extracts only that layer's volume.

FINDING 3 — STRIP FOOTINGS WITHOUT BASEQUANTITIES REQUIRED GEOMETRIC EXTRACTION
18 strip footings. No BaseQuantities exported. This is a known Revit IFC exporter limitation — foundation elements do not receive BaseQuantities regardless of export settings.
Initial engine applied uniform defaults — 4.00m length, 0.60m width, 0.30m depth — to all 18 elements identically.
Reality from geometry — IfcExtrudedAreaSolid representations present on every footing. Lengths ranging from 0.049m connector pieces to 18.275m perimeter runs. Some footings with up to 11 separate extrusion segments.
Geometry-derived volumes — 18 individual values ranging from 0.048 m³ to 2.384 m³. Total — 16.591 m³.
Default-based result — 12.960 m³.
Additional issue — element name says 600×200mm Strip Footing. Width 600mm. Depth 200mm. Engine was applying default depth of 300mm. Wrong by 50%.
The geometry was in the file. It was being ignored entirely.

FINDING 4 — PAD FOOTINGS MISCLASSIFIED AS FLOOR SLABS
41 pad footings exported as IfcSlab with PredefinedType BASESLAB. The Revit modeler used the Floor category instead of the Footing category. When exported, Revit assigned the slab class.
Two distinct sizes confirmed by script.
B1 — 9 elements — 1200×1200×300mm — 0.432 m³ each
B2 — 32 elements — 1000×1000×300mm — 0.300 m³ each
PredefinedType combined with name-based override reclassified all 41 to footing before any steel ratio or cost logic was applied.
This pattern repeats across multiple Revit datasets. It is not a Revit bug. It is a modeling practice. The detection must be in the engine.

FINDING 5 — GROSSVOLUME UNIT INCONSISTENCY IN IFC2X3 EXPORT
Multiple elements show corrupted GrossVolume.
BASESLAB B1 — GrossVolume 432.0 m³, NetVolume 0.432 m³ — factor of 1000.
Spandrel beams — GrossVolume mismatch approximately 260×.
NetVolume is consistent across all affected elements. GrossVolume is unreliable in specific IFC2X3 Revit exports. This is a systematic export bug, not a modeling error.
NetVolume used exclusively throughout. GrossVolume ignored unless cross-validated against geometry.

VALIDATION PIPELINE
IfcReinforcingBar full geometry extraction — CrossSectionArea × BarLength × 7850 per individual bar
Multi-layer slab decomposition via IfcMaterialLayerSetUsage — structural layer isolated
Strip footing extraction from IfcExtrudedAreaSolid — segment aggregation per element
BASESLAB reclassification to footing before any cost or steel logic
NetVolume-first policy across all elements
Name-based dimension parsing — fallback only when geometry absent
IFC2X3 quantity handling — VolumeValue and AreaValue access throughout
Element-level reconciliation in Bonsai/BlenderBIM
Full traceability per element

THE RESULT
Before correction — concrete 166.916 m³, steel 20,906 kg.
After correction — concrete 122.801 m³, steel 2,409.84 kg.
Bonsai ground truth — 122.201 m³.
StructBOQ after fixes — 122.801 m³.
Delta — 0.603 m³ — floating point rounding across 18 geometry-extracted strip footings.
Real discrepancies — zero.
Cost impact — €38,104 to €29,489. A difference of €8,615 on a single residential project from extraction errors that produced no warning and no flag in the original output.

ONE HONEST LIMITATION
18 strip footings extracted from IFC geometry due to missing BaseQuantities. Confidence 85%. Verify against structural drawings before procurement.
One ground beam — GBM:1818908 — had NetVolume = 0.0. Volume reconstructed from Pset length and name-parsed dimensions. Confidence 70%. Verify against structural drawings.
Steel quantities are exact for all 1,252 modeled bars. No estimation applied.

WHY THIS MATTERS
The failure was not data absence. It was unread data.
1,252 bars fully modeled but ignored.
46.842 m³ of non-structural layers counted as concrete.
Strip footings replaced by uniform assumptions.
Pad footings accepted under the wrong IFC class.
GrossVolume trusted where it should not have been.
Nothing failed visibly. Everything looked correct. The BOQ was wrong anyway.
IFC errors are rarely missing data problems. They are detection and interpretation failures.
And in real delivery environments, speed of correction matters as much as correctness itself — because wrong numbers that arrive confidently still become decisions.
